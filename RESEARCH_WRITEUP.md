# Measuring and Evaluating AI-Generated Content (AIGC) on TikTok: A Multi-Signal Detection Study

## 1. Introduction

The rapid proliferation of AI-generated content (AIGC) across social media platforms raises critical questions about transparency, disclosure, and content authenticity. As generative AI tools such as Sora, Runway, Kling, Midjourney, and others become increasingly accessible, it is essential to understand how platforms like TikTok label such content and whether creators voluntarily disclose AI involvement.

This study investigates the prevalence, disclosure patterns, and engagement characteristics of AI-generated content on TikTok. We employ a multi-signal detection framework that combines three independent indicators: TikTok's built-in platform labeling, creator self-disclosure (via hashtags and caption text), and human expert judgment. The analysis is conducted across two distinct datasets to ensure robustness and generalizability.

---

## 2. Methodology

### 2.1 Data Collection

We assembled two datasets to examine AIGC from complementary perspectives:

| Property | Dataset 1: Random API Sample | Dataset 2: Manually Labeled Database |
|----------|------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Source** | TikTok API metadata harvest | Manual curation and human labeling |
| **Size** | **10,200 videos** | **86 videos** (with metadata for 2,865 videos) |
| **Collection method** | Automated API queries | Researchers watched and labeled each video |
| **Manual labels** | None (no human review) | Yes -- each video labeled as "AI" or "NOT AI" |
| **Metadata source** | 10,200 individual JSON files | 84 JSON files via Selenium page scraping |
| **Time span** | Covers pre-AI and post-AI eras | Covers pre-AI and post-AI eras |

### 2.2 Temporal Segmentation

We define a temporal boundary at **February 1, 2024** -- the approximate date of OpenAI's Sora announcement, which marked a significant inflection point for public AI video generation tools.

- **Pre-AI era**: Videos created before 2024-02-01
- **Post-AI era**: Videos created on or after 2024-02-01

### 2.3 Multi-Signal Detection Framework

Our analysis tracks AI content through **three orthogonal detection signals**:

#### Signal 1: Platform Signal (TikTok's Official Label)
TikTok embeds AI-related metadata within its page JSON. We extract:
- `IsAigc` / `is_aigc` boolean flag
- `AIGCLabelType` badge information
- `ai_gc_description` field

If any of these indicate AI content, we classify the video as platform-labeled AIGC.

#### Signal 2: Creator Signal (Self-Disclosure)
We detect creator self-disclosure through two channels:

**a) Hashtag Analysis** -- We monitor **52 AI-related hashtags** organized in the following categories:

**Generic AI terms (16):** #aigc, #ai, #aiart, #aigenerated, #aicreated, #aiartwork, #aianimation, #aivideo, #aifilm, #aiclip, #aicontentcreator, #generativeai, #syntheticmedia, #artificialintelligence, #ai_generated, #ai_art

**Video generation tools (18):** #sora, #soraai, #runway, #runwayml, #kling, #klingai, #pika, #pikaart, #pikalabs, #heygen, #heygenai, #luma, #lumaai, #dreamina, #haiper, #invideo, #morphstudio, #genmo

**Image generation tools (4):** #midjourney, #stablediffusion, #dalle, #dalle3

**Broader AI mentions (11):** #chatgpt, #openai, #aiavatar, #aiedit, #aiediting, #aitools, #aitool, #aifilter, #aifilters, #deepfake, #deepfakes

**Additional hashtags tracked in Dataset 2 parser (3):** #deepfake, #chatgpt, #openai (overlap with above, plus the bare #ai tag used in both)

**b) Caption Text Analysis** -- We apply regex pattern matching to detect AI-related phrases in video captions:
- Direct declarations: "AI generated", "AI created", "AI made", "AI art", "AI video", "AI animation"
- Attribution phrases: "made with AI", "created by AI", "generated by AI", "created using AI"
- Disclosure statements: "this is AI", "not real", "digitally created", "synthetic media", "AIGC"

#### Signal 3: Manual Signal (Human Expert Labels)
In Dataset 2, human researchers viewed each video and assigned a binary label: AI or NOT AI. This serves as the ground-truth reference for evaluating automated detection accuracy.

#### Additional Diagnostic: Broad AI Text Signal
For Dataset 1, we apply a broader heuristic scan to detect any mention of AI-related terms, tool names (e.g., Sora, Midjourney, DALL-E, Stable Diffusion, Runway, Kling, Pika, HeyGen, Luma), or AI-adjacent phrases (e.g., deepfake, face swap, text-to-video, image-to-video) in video captions.

### 2.4 Agreement Analysis

We compute inter-signal agreement to measure consistency:
- **Overall agreement**: Percentage of videos where two signals give the same answer
- **Positive agreement**: Among cases where *at least one* signal says "AI", the percentage where *both* agree
- **Disclosure gap**: Cases where the creator tagged content as AI but the platform did *not* flag it

### 2.5 Engagement Metrics

For each video, we extract and compute:
- **Play count** (views)
- **Like count**
- **Comment count**
- **Share count**
- **Like rate** = likes / views
- **Comment rate** = comments / views
- **Engagement total** = views + likes + comments + shares

All engagement comparisons use **median values** to reduce the influence of viral outliers.

## 3. Findings

### 3.1 AI-Generated Content Prevalence

#### Dataset 1: Random API Sample (10,200 videos)

| Signal | Count Flagged | Total | Prevalence | 95% CI |
|--------|--------------|-------|------------|--------|
| Platform raw `is_aigc` | 0 | 10,200 | **0.00%** | 0.00 -- 0.04% |
| Platform derived label | 0 | 10,200 | **0.00%** | 0.00 -- 0.04% |
| Creator AI-tag (hashtags/caption) | 1 | 10,200 | **0.01%** | 0.00 -- 0.06% |
| Disclosure gap | 1 | 10,200 | **0.01%** | 0.00 -- 0.06% |
| Broad AI text signal (any AI mention) | 9 | 10,200 | **0.09%** | 0.05 -- 0.17% |
| Union signal (platform OR creator OR text) | 10 | 10,200 | **0.10%** | 0.05 -- 0.18% |

**Key finding**: In a random sample of 10,200 TikTok videos, TikTok's platform did not flag a single video as AI-generated. Only 1 creator self-identified their content as AI-made. Even with the broadest possible heuristic (any mention of AI-related terms), only 10 videos (0.10%) showed any AI signal.

#### Dataset 2: Manually Labeled Database (2,865 videos)

| Creator AI-tag (hashtags/caption) | 0 | 2,865 | **0.00%** | 0.00 -- 0.13% |
| Disclosure gap | 0 | 2,865 | **0.00%** | 0.00 -- 0.13% |

**Key finding**: TikTok's raw `is_aigc` flag returned false for all 2,865 videos. However, derived platform signals (badge type, AI description fields) identified 5 videos (0.17%) as AIGC. No creators used any of the 52 tracked AI hashtags or AI-related caption phrases.

#### Overall Agreement Rates

| Comparison | Dataset 1 (10,200) | Dataset 2 (2,865) |
|------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Platform vs. Creator | **99.99%** | **99.83%** |
| Positive agreement (among AI-positive cases) | **0.00%** (n=1) | **0.00%** (n=5) |
| Platform vs. Manual | N/A | N/A (n=0) |
| Creator vs. Manual | N/A | N/A (n=0) |

**Key finding**: Overall agreement rates appear exceptionally high (>99.8%), but this is an artifact of extreme class imbalance -- both signals are almost always "NOT AI." The more meaningful metric is **positive agreement**, which is **0.00%** in both datasets. This means: whenever one signal did detect AI, the other signal did *not* confirm it. The platform and creator signals never agreed that a specific video was AI-generated.

## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 The AIGC Detection Gap

Our most striking finding is the near-total absence of AI labeling on TikTok. Across 10,200 random videos, TikTok's platform flagged **zero** videos as AI-generated. Even in our curated dataset of 2,865 videos, only **5 (0.17%)** were detected through derived platform signals. This raises several possibilities:

1. **AI content is genuinely rare**: The vast majority of TikTok content may still be organic, human-created content.
2. **Detection mechanisms are insufficient**: TikTok's `is_aigc` flag appears to be non-functional or not widely deployed -- it returned `false` for every single video across both datasets.
3. **AI content evades detection**: Sophisticated AI-generated videos may not trigger platform detection mechanisms.

### 4.2 Creator Disclosure is Nearly Non-Existent

Despite tracking 52 AI-related hashtags and multiple caption-text patterns, creator self-disclosure was effectively zero. Only 1 out of 10,200 videos in the random sample included any AI-related hashtag. This suggests:

- Creators have **no incentive** to disclose AI usage
- There is **no cultural norm** around AI content transparency on TikTok
- Even creators using AI tools may **not perceive** their content as "AI-generated"

### 4.3 Signal Disagreement

The 0% positive agreement between platform and creator signals is particularly concerning. In the rare cases where AI was detected by one signal, the other signal did not confirm it. This means the two primary detection mechanisms are **not corroborating each other**, undermining confidence in either method alone.

### 4.4 The Engagement Paradox

AI-labeled content showed a paradoxical engagement pattern: higher views but dramatically lower like rates. This could indicate:
- **Algorithmic amplification**: TikTok may push AI content for distribution but audiences respond less favorably
- **Curiosity vs. appreciation**: Users view AI content out of curiosity but don't engage positively
- **Sample size limitation**: With only 5 AI-labeled videos, this pattern requires validation with larger samples

### 4.5 Temporal Trends

The consistent ~35% increase in views across both datasets in the post-AI era (after Feb 2024) is noteworthy but likely reflects broader platform growth rather than AI-specific effects, since the AI labeling rate remained near zero in both eras.

---

## 5. Limitations

1. **Small AI-positive sample sizes**: Only 5 platform-labeled AI videos and 1 creator-tagged AI video were found. Statistical conclusions about AI content characteristics are limited.
2. **No ground truth for Dataset 1**: The random API sample lacks human labels, so we cannot evaluate detection accuracy (false negatives).
3. **Scraping limitations**: Some videos in the manually labeled dataset used shortened URLs that could not be resolved (status: `no_video_id_in_url`).
4. **Temporal snapshot**: Data represents a single collection period and may not reflect evolving platform policies.
5. **Language bias**: AI hashtags and caption patterns are English-centric and may miss non-English disclosures.
6. **Platform API changes**: TikTok's metadata structure and AI labeling fields may change over time, affecting data consistency.

---

## 6. Conclusions

This study provides empirical evidence of a significant **transparency gap** in AI-generated content on TikTok:

- **TikTok's automated AI detection is functionally non-operative** in our samples, with the raw `is_aigc` flag returning false for all 13,065 videos examined.
- **Creator self-disclosure is virtually absent**, with only 1 out of 10,200 randomly sampled videos including any AI-related hashtag.
- **Platform and creator signals never corroborate each other** (0% positive agreement), suggesting that when AI content exists, neither mechanism reliably catches it.
- **AI-labeled content shows distinct engagement patterns** -- higher views but 4x lower like rates -- hinting at audience skepticism toward AI content.
- **The post-AI era (post-Feb 2024) brought higher overall engagement** (~35% more views) but did not meaningfully increase AI disclosure rates.

These findings suggest that current AI content governance on TikTok -- relying on platform automation and voluntary creator disclosure -- is **insufficient for meaningful transparency**. Independent detection methods (such as visual AI classifiers) are needed to complement existing mechanisms.
